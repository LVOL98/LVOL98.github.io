### The Kyosanim Program

Though this is an article about learning, a substantial part of that is how the teaching is structured. The way a SSH black belt is taught to teach in the official curriculum is the following 5 different levels:

1. Technique (learning)
2. Spirit (After though and understanding)
3. Hearth (Feel, understand with the hearth, to be enlightened)
4. To do (Doing, realise, practice (to use what you have learned))
5. Live (Live with joy)

Now lets express that in simpler terms:

1. What your body does
2. Understanding the benefits and drawbacks
3. Noticing point 2 while doing a technique
4. Using it
5. Joy

People unfamiliar with SSH will probably be quite confused about the last 2 points, but they come as techniques in SSH do also include 'sharing something with others' (food, water etc.) and a deeper meaning with the techniques (our feet are a symbol of the earth, so by practicing kicks we get a better connection with the earth).

While the above are important to the teachings they're not really relevant for this article, hence why they will not be elaborated on and only points 1-3 will be covered.

From reading this one might intuitively conclude that this is a separation of techniques which might very well be the case, but it's something that both teachers and students forget again and again, as it's to be frank, boring. The goal of this exercise is to learn the technique, and actually doing a kick instead of talking about which way the foot is turning is more interesting for both parties. This becomes apparent later on once the student have to do a front kick to the head, and the force their leg to head height compromising both their upper body, (by bending their back) and putting a strain on their hip by not turning their foot probably.

## The Methodology, Learning by subdivision

### The algorithmic way

Let's start by expressing it in algorithmic terms and then in a more understandable way. The basic idea is to look at the most essential part of any technique: the movement. Now all movements starts from the legs as the body can only lean without moving the legs, and leaning usually doesn't cause confusion in learning. After having defined the movements of the legs, the movements of the hands are defined. Alas the leg movements and the hand movements are combined and any rotations are dealt with. Now the algorithm

```python
Decomposit the movement(legs, hands):
    What does the legs do:
        Initial stance
        What leg moves
        if (leg crosses other leg):
            Does it cross in front of
            Does it cross behind
        # Note: rotations are dealt with later
        
    What does the hands do:
        Initial positions
        Which hand moves
        if (hand crosses other hand):
            Does it cross in front of
            Does it cross behind
            Does it cross over
            Does it cross under
        if (hand touches opponent):
            if (block):
                Does it grab:
                    Inside or outside
                Does it push/pull:
                    Away or up or down
    
Perform the technique(Decomposition of the movement):
    Do leg movement
    Do hand movement
    if (rotation):
        if (legs crossed):
            Turn until legs are uncrossed
        if (legs not crossed):
            Turn head to the given direction
```

### The simplification

Now this might seem a bit overwhelming and very comprehensive and not very readable if you're not familiar with programming. The reason why this seems so overwhelming is that it has to take into account all possible inputs, but one could express this in three lines:

1. What does the legs do
2. What does the hands do
3. Combine legs and hands with rotations

By just having this in the back of the head some techniques becomes a lot easier to grasp, but this short explanation also abstract away some important observations, so a deep dive is needed.

#### 1. What does the legs do

By far the most important of the three as this can deal with rotations in techniques, which causes the most confusion when learning a new technique. During this phase the only thing one need to remember is:

- What leg moved?
- Where did it move to?
- Did it cross the other leg?

By noting this rotations can be dealt with later on, without even having to know if it's clockwise or counter clockwise. Also by first focusing on the leg movement, usually what does hand does comes more naturally as its movements are limited.
