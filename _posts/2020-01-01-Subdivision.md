## Overview

Before heading into the essence of this article, a short discussion about a methodology focusing on analytical separating of things/subjects will be measured against a holistic methodology. This will be followed with a short overview of the purposed system of learning given in the Kyosanim and 2. Dan curriculum, with a focus of where to apply this methodology. All this leads to the discussion of a method of learning which analytically separates techniques into their essence, followed by a holistic union of the sub techniques identified in the analytical breakdown

### The failings of the holistic and "western analytical" compartmentalizing thoughts

Both use each other at their core, otherwise both would fail

A clear point was set in the theoretical curriculum from 1. Dan to 2. Dan:

> "This nature conscious attitude to life and the universe (**Shinson**) that in Asia has affected areas like taoism, buddhism and confucianism in a meaningful way, separates itself mainly from the western analytical thinking by having a tendency to see the importance in the collection and 'wholeness' of things, more than their own special conditions. The basic ideas in this holistic philosophy is also the basis in asian nature therapy" [^1]

This quote can be interpreted to put emphasis on the superiority of the holistic approach in regards to Hwal In Sul[^2], even though this probably never was the intention. The reason why this specific quote is taken out is because it symbolizes a reoccurring theme in Shinson Hapkido (*SSH*), the focus and emphasis on the holistic approach. The general though that is given is

> "Our ego exists by separating, dividing and judging. Thus we create the world of opposites (**Um and Yang**) where we live" [^3]

Which again emphasizes that we should rather see the whole rather than the subparts, fx. rather than seeing plants and animals we should see them all as living beings. While this though is important (especially later in the learning process) it does have a tendency to be more dominant than the 'western analytical thinking'. The fallacies connected to the previous quote will not be elaborated on here, but rather in another article (LINK: will be here). Though I (the author) will be biased for a more 'western analytical' approach, I do appreciate and understand the importance of a holistic methodology, as will be apparent later in this article

The takeaway in this short discussion is that the focus of learning leans towards the holistic total view of the techniques and learning them. Instead this article will argue that the focus in the start should lean towards a breakdown of techniques to further the understanding, and later on once the sub techniques should be put together to a whole the holistic focus returns, and comes more naturally (TODO: claim not supported)

In software engineering/development the old goal of testing is

> "To break the program"

Summary: focus on edge cases and all possible movements/actions, not to find the right and wrongs, but to find the strength and more important the weakness of all movements
